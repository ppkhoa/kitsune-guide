    Converting Lossless Audio Formats with eac3to¶

    Lossless audio formats on Blu-rays and DVDs — such as LPCM, DTS-HD Master Audio, and TrueHD — often contain far more data than necessary for mono (1.0) or stereo (2.0) mixes.
    Using eac3to, you can safely convert these tracks to FLAC without any quality loss, dramatically reducing file size and improving compatibility.

    This guide explains why conversion is recommended, when to avoid it, and how to perform it correctly.

    Why Convert Lossless Audio to FLAC?¶

    Reduce File Size (No Quality Loss)¶

    • LPCM → Completely uncompressed → huge files
    • DTS-HD MA 1.0/2.0 → HD layer provides little value
    • TrueHD 1.0/2.0 → Can be efficiently stored as FLAC

    FLAC preserves 100% of the signal while significantly lowering file size.

    Improve Compatibility¶

    Many editing programs cannot import: - DTS-HD MA - TrueHD - LPCM wrapped in M2TS/MKV

    FLAC and WAV are universally supported.

    Tracker-Approved¶

    All major private trackers allow: - FLAC 1.0 for mono
    - FLAC 2.0 for stereo

    These are transparent and preferred unless the source is multichannel.

    When NOT to Convert¶

    Do not convert these formats when they are 5.1 or higher: - DTS-HD Master Audio 5.1+ - TrueHD 5.1+ (especially Atmos) - Multichannel LPCM

    Lossless multichannel should be preserved as-is.

    Never convert lossy → lossless: - AC-3 → FLAC = pointless
    - DTS core → FLAC = pointless

    Why Multichannel TrueHD Should Not Be Converted to FLAC¶

    While FLAC is lossless, converting multichannel TrueHD (5.1+) to FLAC is not equivalent preservation and is strongly discouraged.

    TrueHD can contain discrete channel layouts and metadata that are lost when converting to FLAC, including:

    • Dolby-specific channel mapping
    • Dynamic Range Control (DRC) metadata
    • Dialogue normalization (dialnorm)
    • Atmos-related signaling (when present)

    Although FLAC preserves the raw PCM samples, it cannot carry Dolby metadata, meaning the converted track is no longer functionally identical to the source.

    Additionally, multichannel FLAC has poor hardware and software compatibility: - Many TVs, AV receivers, and streaming devices do not support FLAC 5.1+ - Playback often falls back to stereo or fails entirely - TrueHD is far more widely supported in home theater environments

    For these reasons: - TrueHD 5.1+ must always be preserved - Only TrueHD 1.0 / 2.0 should ever be converted to FLAC

    Inspecting Audio Tracks¶

    Run:

    eac3to input.mkv

    Example output:

    1: h264/AVC
2: DTS-HD Master Audio, English, 2.0
3: LPCM, Japanese, 1.0

    Take note of the track ID for conversion.

    Converting LPCM → FLAC¶

    eac3to input.mkv 3: output.flac

    Converting DTS-HD MA → FLAC (1.0 / 2.0 Only)¶

    eac3to input.mkv 2: output.flac

    ⚠️ DTS-HD MA 1.0 has archival value,
    but re-encoding requires proprietary DTS tools.
    Thus FLAC 1.0 is preferred for releases.

    Converting TrueHD → FLAC¶

    eac3to input.mkv 2: output.flac

    Optional: Converting to WAV for Editing¶

    eac3to input.mkv 2: output.wav

    Verification¶

    mediainfo output.flac

    Expected: - Channels: 1 or 2
    - Codec: FLAC

    Summary Table¶

    Source Format Output Notes
    LPCM 1.0 / 2.0 FLAC 1.0 / 2.0 Always convert
    DTS-HD MA 1.0 / 2.0 FLAC 1.0 / 2.0 Preferred to convert on most sites
    TrueHD 1.0 / 2.0 FLAC 1.0 / 2.0 Preferred to convert on most sites
    DTS-HD MA 5.1+ Keep original Do not convert
    TrueHD 5.1+ (Atmos) Keep original Do not convert
    AC-3 / DTS (lossy) Keep original Never re-encode
