    Video Codecs & Containers¶

    Understanding how video is encoded and stored is essential for handling WEB-DLs, encodes, remuxes, and professional media workflows. This section breaks down modern video codecs, legacy formats, and container formats, explaining what they do and when they're used.

    Video Compression Formats¶

    Video codecs determine how video is compressed, how much detail is preserved, and what devices can play it. Below are the most widely used codecs today.

    H.264 (Advanced Video Coding, AVC)¶

    Released: 2003
    Developed by: ITU-T & ISO/IEC

    Key Features:
    - Motion-compensated, block-based compression
    - High efficiency at low bitrates
    - Supports resolutions up to 8K UHD

    8-bit: Universal support across all devices
    10-bit: Higher precision, reduced banding (Hi10P)

    H.265 (High Efficiency Video Coding, HEVC)¶

    Released: 2013
    Developed by: ITU-T & ISO/IEC

    Key Features:
    - 25–50% better compression vs H.264
    - Supports 8K UHD
    - Variable block segmentation for higher efficiency

    8-bit: Efficient streaming
    10-bit: Required for HDR

    H.266 (Versatile Video Coding, VVC)¶

    Released: 2020
    Developed by: ITU-T & ISO/IEC

    Key Features:
    - ~50% better compression than HEVC
    - Supports 16K, HDR, 360° video

    8-bit: Modern baseline codec
    10-bit: Best for HDR and high-end workflows

    AV1 (AOMedia Video 1)¶

    Released: 2018
    Developed by: Alliance for Open Media

    Key Features:
    - Royalty-free
    - Superior compression to HEVC and VP9
    - Designed for internet streaming

    8-bit: Efficient and open
    10-bit: HDR-capable with improved color depth

    VP9¶

    Released: 2013
    Developed by: Google

    Key Features:
    - Successor to VP8
    - Royalty-free competitor to HEVC
    - Primary YouTube codec

    8-bit: Common for web video
    10-bit: Offers HDR support and better efficiency

    MPEG-4¶

    Released: 1998
    Developed by: ISO/IEC MPEG

    Key Features:
    - Includes MPEG-4 Part 2 and Part 10 (H.264)
    - Early format for internet video and broadcast

    8-bit: Standard usage
    10-bit: Rarely used

    MPEG-2¶

    Released: 1995
    Developed by: ISO/IEC MPEG

    Key Features:
    - Used for DVDs and digital TV broadcast
    - Less efficient than modern codecs

    8-bit: Standard for legacy media
    10-bit: Not commonly implemented

    Video Containers¶

    Video containers store video, audio, chapters, subtitles, and metadata. They do not determine compression — only how streams are packaged.

    MP4 (MPEG-4 Part 14)¶

    Released: 2001
    Developed by: ISO/IEC MPEG

    Key Features:
    - Stores video, audio, subtitles, and stills
    - Excellent streaming support
    - Very high device compatibility

    MKV (Matroska Video)¶

    Released: 2002
    Developed by: Matroska

    Key Features:
    - Open-standard container
    - Supports multiple video, audio, and subtitle tracks
    - Future-proof format used for WEB-DLs, encodes, and remuxes

    Summary¶

    Key Takeaways

    • Codecs control compression and quality (H.264, H.265, AV1, etc.)
    • 10-bit encoding improves color and is required for HDR
    • MKV is the preferred container for WEB-DL workflows
    • MP4 is the most universally compatible container
    • New codecs like AV1 and VVC offer the best long-term efficiency
