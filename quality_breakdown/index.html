    Quality Breakdown of Streaming Services¶

    A practical overview of what WEB-DL handlers can expect from major platforms.

    Different streaming providers vary in bitrate, encoding strategy, audio handling, subtitle formatting, and overall consistency.
    This page focuses on technical behavior that matters when creating, comparing, or cleaning WEB-DLs.

    Crunchyroll¶

    Crunchyroll WEB-DLs are primarily anime-focused releases with very consistent technical characteristics.

    Video¶

    • Resolutions: Commonly available in 720p, 1080p, and 1440p.
      Older catalog titles may also exist in SD-only variants.

    • Codec: H.264 / AVC exclusively for all official Crunchyroll streams.
      Any HEVC (H.265) releases are third-party re-encodes, not official WEB-DLs.

    • Frame rate:

    • 23.976 FPS for most content (standard for anime)

    • 29.970 FPS (NTSC) for some older or region-specific titles

    • Bitrate behavior: All Crunchyroll WEB-DLs use Variable Bitrate (VBR) encoding.

    • 1440p: typically ~6,500 kb/s
    • 1080p: typically around 8,000 kb/s

    • 720p: typically around 4,000 kb/s

    • Common artifacts:

    • Banding in gradients
    • Compression noise in dark scenes
    • Occasional ringing around high-contrast line art

    These artifacts are common due to Crunchyroll’s bitrate constraints and animation-heavy content.

    Audio¶

    • Primary codec: AAC-LC 2.0
    • Typical bitrate: 128–192 kb/s
    • Multi-audio: Multiple language tracks are common (JP + dubs).
    • Surround audio: 5.1 mixes are effectively nonexistent on official Crunchyroll streams.

    Any CR release advertising HEVC or 5.1 audio should be treated as re-encoded or non-official.

    Subtitles¶

    • Crunchyroll provides soft subtitles, typically:
    • Styled .ass subtitles for legacy content
    • Simplified .ass or .srt for newer pipelines
    • Subtitles may be:
    • Embedded in the container
    • Delivered as separate files
    • Common cleanup tasks:
    • LayoutRes / PlayRes corrections
    • Font attachment (especially for older CR typesets)
    • Styling and sign restoration

    Amazon Prime Video¶

    Amazon is one of the most important WEB-DL sources and has multiple distinct streaming pipelines.

    1080p Video (AVC/H.264)¶

    • Two main behaviors often observed:
    • Streams resembling constant bitrate (CBR-like) around the 8–10 Mb/s range.
    • Streams with more variable behavior up to roughly 12–15 Mb/s peak.
    • Even when the nominal peak bitrate is lower, some of the more constrained streams can still look cleaner than competitors due to Amazon’s encoder tuning.
    • Cropping can differ slightly between pipelines, but aspect ratio is preserved.

    2160p Video (UHD)¶

    Amazon uses different manifest types for UHD:

    • ISM-based deliveries typically peak around the high teens (roughly up to 20 Mb/s).
    • MPD-based deliveries often have a slightly different bitrate envelope (commonly up to the mid-teens for HDR, higher for SDR in some cases).
    • It is not uncommon to see:
    • One pipeline with higher nominal bitrate but slightly less efficient compression.
    • Another with lower peak bitrate but noticeably better perceptual quality.
    • HDR:
    • HDR10 is common.
    • Dolby Vision is usually reserved for Amazon Originals and select licensed titles.

    Typical Issues¶

    • Some titles, especially dark, grainy, or heavily graded content, can have 1080p streams in the 3–4 Mb/s range, which leads to:
    • Crushed shadow detail.
    • Banding and contouring.
    • Certain masters show a slight green push or squeezed look compared to physical media, but streaming quality is still often superior to many competitors at the same resolution.

    Audio¶

    • Common formats:
    • Dolby Digital Plus (E-AC-3) 5.1 at or near 640 kb/s.
    • DDP 2.0 often in the low-to-mid 200 kb/s range.
    • Dolby Atmos is mostly present on first-party content and select premium titles; bitrates are lower than disc but still adequate for streaming.
    • For WEB-DLs, Amazon audio is often preferred if available in higher-bitrate 5.1 or Atmos.

    Netflix¶

    Heavily optimized for bandwidth efficiency, sometimes at the cost of fine detail.

    Video¶

    • Codecs: AV1/HEVC for many modern devices; AVC still present on older or constrained platforms.
    • Bitrate:
    • 1080p is often in the 4–8 Mb/s range depending on title and device.
    • 2160p HDR typically in the 12–16 Mb/s band, but heavily content-adaptive.
    • Uses complex per-shot and per-title encoding, so quality can vary scene-by-scene.
    • Common artifacts:
    • Detail “pulsing” when the encoder reallocates bits between shots.
    • Macroblocking and posterization in very dark scenes.

    HDR and Dolby Vision¶

    • Netflix is an aggressive adopter of HDR and DV.
    • DV is common for high-profile originals and many licensed titles.
    • HDR grades are usually consistent, but tone-mapping can differ between device classes.

    Audio¶

    • Dolby Digital Plus 5.1 is standard for most premium titles.
    • Dolby Atmos offered on select content, usually at relatively conservative bitrates compared to disc.
    • Dialog clarity is generally fine, but dynamic range can be narrower than on UHD Blu-ray.

    Disney+¶

    Disney+ is generally stable and predictable, especially for UHD.

    Video¶

    • 1080p:
    • Bitrate commonly in the 4–8 Mb/s range.
    • Many catalog titles look soft, either due to older masters or heavy noise reduction.
    • 2160p:
    • HDR10 widely used; Dolby Vision on select titles.
    • Bitrates often in the low-to-mid teens for UHD.
    • Pixar and modern Disney titles are usually very clean, with careful grain management.

    Audio¶

    • Dolby Digital Plus 5.1 is typical.
    • Atmos available on many big releases (Marvel, Star Wars, etc.).
    • Some catalog titles retain original mixes but at lower bitrates.

    Common Quirks¶

    • Older films may have heavy DNR, altered grain structure, or revisionist grading.
    • Occasionally, localized versions use different masters.

    HBO Max¶

    Refers to the current, rebranded streaming service carrying HBO and Warner content.

    Video¶

    • 1080p:
    • Bitrates typically mid-range, often above some lower-tier services but below Apple or the best Amazon encodes.
    • HBO originals are usually sourced from strong masters, so they still look good even with moderate bitrate.
    • 2160p:
    • Available on a subset of high-profile titles.
    • HDR10 and sometimes Dolby Vision on certain releases.
    • Overall encode quality:
    • Better than many regional platforms.
    • Not as aggressively tuned as Apple TV+ for pristine detail.

    Audio¶

    • Dolby Digital Plus 5.1 is standard for modern content.
    • Atmos support exists for certain titles but is less pervasive than on Disney+ or Apple TV+.
    • Dialogue-forward mixes; dynamic range can vary.

    Notes¶

    • HBO-originated shows often look very good relative to their nominal bitrates.
    • Library titles are hit-or-miss depending on which master was used.

    Apple TV+¶

    Apple prioritizes visual fidelity and is often considered a reference streaming platform.

    Video¶

    • Codecs: HEVC (H.265) for nearly all video; AV1 is not widely used as of now.
    • 1080p:
    • Bitrates commonly higher than typical competitors at the same resolution.
    • 2160p:
    • Average bitrates are frequently in the 20 Mb/s range or higher for high-motion or complex material.
    • Encodes are extremely clean with minimal macroblocking.
    • HDR:
    • Heavy use of Dolby Vision along with HDR10.
    • Tone mapping and color reproduction are usually excellent.

    Audio¶

    • Dolby Digital Plus 5.1 for most devices.
    • Dolby Atmos on a large portion of original content.
    • Spatial and dynamic mixes are generally very strong for streaming.

    Peacock¶

    Quality varies a lot depending on whether the title is an original or a deep-catalog import.

    Video¶

    • 1080p:
    • Bitrates often in the 3–6 Mb/s range.
    • New originals look acceptable; older shows may look flat or noisy.
    • UHD:
    • Limited selection; not as widely deployed as on the larger services.
    • Artifacts:
    • Compression in fast motion.
    • Some masters appear to be older HD broadcast encodes.

    Audio¶

    • AC-3 or E-AC-3 2.0/5.1 depending on title.
    • Rarely a top choice for audio quality compared to Amazon or Apple.

    NOW and SkyShowtime¶

    Primarily European services tied to regional rights and shared pipelines.

    Video¶

    • Often share masters or encodes similar to Peacock and Paramount+.
    • Bitrates are typically on the lower side, especially for 1080p.
    • Expect visibly compressed streams, particularly on older catalog content.

    Audio¶

    • 2.0 and 5.1 mixes, usually at modest bitrates.
    • Rarely a first-choice source when better alternatives exist in other regions.

    Hulu¶

    A hybrid library of network TV, originals, and licensed content.

    Video¶

    • 1080p:
    • Bitrates in the 3–6 Mb/s range are common.
    • Modern originals are acceptable; some network-sourced content looks like upscaled or noisy HD.
    • UHD:
    • Present on a limited selection of titles; not the main focus of the service.

    Audio¶

    • Mostly 2.0 or 5.1, usually AC-3 or E-AC-3 at moderate bitrates.
    • Adequate for casual viewing but rarely the best available mix if another provider carries the same title.

    Paramount+¶

    A mid-tier service with a mix of broadcast, catalog, and original material.

    Video¶

    • 1080p:
    • Bitrates roughly in the 3–6 Mb/s range.
    • Some newer originals look good; others appear soft or heavily processed.
    • 2160p:
    • Offered on a subset of flagship titles.
    • HDR10 is more common than Dolby Vision.
    • Known quirks:
    • Occasional crushed blacks or lifted shadows.
    • Color grading inconsistencies between regions.

    Audio¶

    • 5.1 E-AC-3 is typical.
    • Atmos exists on selected premium titles but is not universal.

    Other Regional Services¶

    Depending on your sourcing, these may also be relevant:

    • iQIYI, VIU (Asia)
    • Hotstar / Disney+ Hotstar (India and related regions)
    • Stan, Binge (Australia)
    • Local European platforms (Canal+, TF1+, etc.)

    Each has its own encoding practices, rights issues, and master sources.
    For serious quality work, always:

    • Compare against known strong sources (Apple TV+, high-bitrate Amazon, disc).
    • Check multiple regions where possible.
    • Document differences in bitrate, cropping, HDR metadata, and audio options.

    Summary¶

    When choosing a streaming source for WEB-DLs:

    • For highest overall fidelity, Apple TV+ and good Amazon encodes are often preferred.
    • Netflix and Disney+ are strong on availability and HDR coverage, but not always on bitrate.
    • HBO Max can be very good for HBO-sourced material, with solid masters at moderate bitrates.
    • Peacock, NOW/SkyShowtime, Hulu, and some regional services are often used when they have exclusives or unique cuts, but they are usually secondary in quality comparisons.

    Use this breakdown together with your Web Tier List and hands-on comparisons to decide which provider should be your primary source for a given title.
