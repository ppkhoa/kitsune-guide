    Understanding Chapters¶

    What Are Chapters?¶

    Chapters are timestamps built into the video container (MKV, MP4, Blu-ray playlist) that mark important points such as:

    • Opening / Intro
    • Ending / Credits
    • Major scenes
    • Recaps
    • Break points
    • Special segments (behind-the-scenes, extras)

    A chapter is typically made up of:

    • A start time
    • A chapter title
    • Optional language tags
    • Optional nested structure (advanced MKV editions)

    Why Chapters Matter¶

    Chapters significantly improve navigation:

    • Let viewers jump to key scenes
    • Make skipping intros/outros easier
    • Help match Blu-ray or theatrical structure
    • Maintain consistency across releases
    • Improve professionalism in remuxes and archival files

    For encoders and remuxers, chapters help restore the intended viewing structure of a release.

    • Uses internal markers
    • Extracted WEB-DLs may or may not contain named chapters

    Hulu / HBO Max / Apple TV+¶

    • Chapter support varies by title

    Crunchyroll¶

    • Typically no chapters, except for movies

    Chapters on Blu-ray¶

    Blu-ray discs always include accurate chapters that reflect:

    • Director-intended breaks
    • Scene transitions
    • Acts in the narrative structure

    These are considered the best source for chapter accuracy and are commonly added to WEB-DL remuxes.

    How Chapters Are Stored¶

    Chapters can be saved in different formats:

    • XML chapters (modern MKV format â€” fully supported)
    • OGM chapters (older, simple key/value format)
    • MPLS chapters (Blu-ray playlists)

    Tools like MKVToolNix allow importing/exporting and editing these formats easily.

    BDInfo¶

    • Inspect Blu-ray structure & chapters

    ChapterEditor¶

    • Convert between XML, OGM, and more
